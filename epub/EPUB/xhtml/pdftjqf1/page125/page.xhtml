CONNECTORS

Using sentence connectors is one of the best ways to improve our writing style. Sentence connectors

are used to express relationships between ideas and to combine sentences.

CONTRAST:  
But, In spite of, Despite, In spite of, However, Nevertheless, Although, Even though, On

the one hand, on the other hand, In contrast, On the contrary

CAUSE AND EFFECT:  
Because, On account of, Because of, Due to, Owing to, Due to the fact that,

Since, Therefore, Thus, Hence, So, Consequently

SHOULD HAVE

We use it to say that a different action was recommended in the past: I  
should have studied  
more.

If you regret a situation: I  
shouldn’t have told  
you that. I’m sorry.

You can also use  
should have / shouldn’t have 
to tell other people that a different action in the past

would have been better: You  
shouldn’t have played 
if your leg hurt.

SECOND CONDITIONAL

Formula: If + simple past, would / could / might + infinitive.

We use it to talk about  
unreal 
or  
imaginary 
situations in the present or the future.

She  
would travel 
all over the world  
if she were 
rich.

She  
would pass 
the exam  
if she ever studied 
.

We also use it to talk about something in the present which is impossible, because it's not true.

If I had  
his number, I  
would call 
him.

If I  
were 
you, I  
wouldn't go 
out with that man.

THIRD CONDITIONAL

Formula: If + past perfect,  
would have 
+ past participle.

It is also called the past unreal conditional or past hypothetical conditional. It is used to imagine a

different past than the one that happened.

If I  
hadn’t eaten  
so much, I  
wouldn’t have gotten 
so fat.

Unit 4

PRESENT PERFECT SIMPLE AND PRESENT PERFECT PROGRESSIVE

We use the present perfect simple tense to express a connection between the past and the present.

He
has collected  
600 stamps. ( 
He started collecting stamps at some time in the past. He may collect

more stamps. 
)

We can use the present perfect progressive tense to express events that started in the past and con-

tinue up to the present or we see the results.

I’ve been decorating 
the house this summer. ( 
The action still continues. 
)

I’ve painted 
the living room blue. ( 
We can see the result now. 
)

The present perfect progressive expresses how long something has been happening. The present

perfect simple expresses how much has been completed.
